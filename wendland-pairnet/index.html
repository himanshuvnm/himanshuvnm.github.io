Structured Sparse Global Mixing

Replacing dense all-pair interactions with geometry-aware compact-support kernels

Interaction Mechanisms

Traditional graph models rely on either dense global mixing or discrete sparsification. These approaches either incur quadratic cost or impose hard, non-smooth truncations.

WendlandPairNet introduces a fundamentally different paradigm: continuous, geometry-aware sparsity induced by compact-support kernels.

DensePairNet mixes all nodes, TopKPairNet selects discrete neighbors, while WendlandPairNet induces smooth, radius-controlled sparsity.

Accuracy–Runtime–Sparsity Tradeoff

Across multiple datasets, WendlandPairNet achieves a balanced operating regime: maintaining competitive accuracy while significantly reducing interaction density.

Unlike TopK selection, sparsity emerges from geometric attenuation rather than discrete pruning, leading to smoother and more stable behavior.

WendlandPairNet occupies a favorable region in the accuracy–runtime–sparsity space across PROTEINS, IMDB-BINARY, and MUTAG datasets.

Kernel-Induced Structure

The Wendland kernel introduces an interpretable control parameter: the interaction radius.

This radius governs the transition between local and global behavior, acting as a continuous knob for model capacity, sparsity, and stability.

The Wendland kernel profile illustrates how interaction strength decays smoothly and vanishes beyond a finite radius.

Interactive Radius Control

The interaction radius acts as a continuous control over locality and sparsity. As the radius increases, interactions expand smoothly rather than appearing abruptly.

Increasing the radius expands the interaction support while preserving smooth decay, illustrating the difference between continuous kernel-based sparsity and discrete neighbor selection.

Takeaway

Dense global interaction is not necessary.

What matters is learning geometrically meaningful interactions.

WendlandPairNet provides a principled path toward structured efficiency in graph learning.
